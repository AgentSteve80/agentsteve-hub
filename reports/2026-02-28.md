## 2. 🌸 Anthropic "Bloom" — Open-Source Framework for Behavioral Evaluation of AI Models (4-Stage Pipeline)
**Source:** Anthropic Research / alignment.anthropic.com / Reddit r/machinelearningnews / MarkTechPost · **Published:** Dec 21, 2025 / gaining traction Feb 2026
**Relevance:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (4/5)
**Tags:** `#ai` `#open-source` `#anthropic` `#alignment` `#evaluation` `#agents` `#claude`

**Bloom** is Anthropic's open-source agentic evaluation framework: you specify a *behavior* (sycophancy, self-preferential bias, deception, over-compliance), and Bloom automatically generates scenarios, runs rollouts, scores frequency and severity, and exports results. It uses a **4-stage pipeline**: Understanding (reads behavior spec) → Ideation (generates test scenarios) → Rollout (executes against live models) → Judgment (scores outputs). Backend: LiteLLM (any model), W&B for sweep tracking, Inspect-compatible transcripts. Anthropic has already run Bloom across **4 alignment behaviors × 16 models** — results track closely with human labels while correctly distinguishing "model organism" misaligned models from production ones.

**So what:** Three direct uses: (1) **Audit our own agent** — we can point Bloom at our OpenClaw/Pal agent setup and measure behaviors we care about: does the agent prioritize completing the task over clarifying ambiguous instructions (over-compliance)? Does it prefer its own outputs over corrections (self-preferential bias)? These are real failure modes in production agentic systems. (2) **Evaluate models before switching** — before replacing Claude Sonnet with DeepSeek V3.2 or Qwen3.5 for cost reasons, run Bloom to characterize behavioral differences. Raw benchmark scores don't capture sycophancy or instruction-following degradation. (3) **Trust verification** — when Pal processes job closeouts and generates invoices autonomously, behavioral consistency matters more than raw capability. Bloom gives us a reproducible audit trail.

## 4. 🔐 Post-Quantum Cryptography: 2026 Is Year One of Mandatory Compliance — What Developers Ship Today Has to Change
**Source:** QuantumXC / NIST / THN "Cybersecurity Predictions 2026" / NISTIR 8547 Draft · **Published:** Feb 2026
**Relevance:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (4/5)
**Tags:** `#security` `#post-quantum` `#cryptography` `#compliance` `#pqc` `#nist` `#tls`

NIST finalized the first three post-quantum cryptography standards in 2024: **ML-KEM** (FIPS 203, formerly Kyber — for key establishment), **ML-DSA** (FIPS 204, formerly Dilithium — for digital signatures), and **SLH-DSA** (FIPS 205, formerly SPHINCS+). **2026 is when the first binding regulatory milestones hit**: EU governments and critical infrastructure operators must have PQC roadmaps and cryptographic inventories completed. US federal agencies are under active migration guidance from NIST. QuantumXC's 2026 predictions: "the first wave of binding PQC compliance requirements" and "vendors unable to commit to 2026–2028 timelines" will face procurement exclusion.

**So what:** This isn't theoretical and it isn't distant. Three practical developer implications: (1) **TLS and auth libraries need PQC-aware updates**. Clerk, Convex, and Vercel all use TLS for auth token transport. When those libraries add PQC cipher suites, we pick it up for free — but only if we're on recent versions. Don't fall behind on `@clerk/nextjs`, `convex`, and Next.js version upgrades — PQC migration is a feature they'll ship silently. (2) **Code signing for EAS builds**: Apple and Google are planning PQC migration for their app signing infrastructure. EAS-signed IPA artifacts will eventually need quantum-resistant signing. This is years away from affecting us, but the migration clock started. (3) **"Harvest now, decrypt later"**: nation-state actors are already recording today's encrypted traffic to decrypt once quantum computers are capable. Any long-lived secrets (API keys, signed certificates) stored in encrypted archives from 2026 forward should be treated as potentially exposed eventually.
